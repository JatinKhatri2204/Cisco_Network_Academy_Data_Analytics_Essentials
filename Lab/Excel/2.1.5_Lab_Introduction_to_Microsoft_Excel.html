Objectives

Background / Scenario

Microsoft Excel is a spreadsheet application developed by Microsoft to run on Windows, Mac OS, Android and iOS. Excel includes calculation and computation capabilities, as well as tools for creating graphs, charts, and pivot tables.

Microsoft provides a free online version of their Office suite at Office.com, which includes Excel. The free version does have some limitations. It does not have the full functionality and features of the paid versions, and it can only be used while connected to the Internet.

There are alternative free spreadsheet applications such as Google Sheets and Apache Calc. While the steps in this lab were created for the free version of Excel from Office.com, other spreadsheet applications have the same functionalities and similar interfaces and can be used to accomplish this work.

Required Resources

Part 1: Accessing the free Microsoft Office Suite

If you do not already have access to Microsoft Excel, follow the steps below to obtain a free version from Office.com.

Step 1: Navigate to Office.com

Step 2: Sign up for the free version of Office

  1. Click the “Sign in” link at the top right of the screen.

Step 3: Log in to your Microsoft account or create one for free

  1. If you already have a Windows, Xbox Live, or Skype login, then you already have an active Microsoft account that you can use; otherwise create a new account.
  2. Log in.
  3. Once logged in, you should see a list of applications that are available in the pane on the left of the window as well as under Create New.

Your Office.com account also includes access to OneDrive, which is an online cloud storage space for the files you create.

Step 4: Launch the Excel app

  1. Once you are logged into your account, click the Excel app icon in the pane on the left.

  2. Next, click on “New blank workbook.”

With a blank workbook now open, you are ready to begin work in Excel. Leave the workbook open and proceed to Part 2 of the lab.

Part 2: Saving and Opening an Excel Workbook

Saving and opening an Excel workbook uses the same process as with any other Microsoft Office application.

The steps below are specific to the free version of Excel accessible through Office.com. If you are using a paid full version of Excel or other spreadsheet program, the exact steps will vary slightly, but the process is essentially the same.

Step 1: Saving an Excel Workbook

  1. To save an Excel workbook, select the File tab in the top left of the menu bar then select Save As in the left pane.

  2. In the Save As options list, select Save a copy to OneDrive.

  3. In the My files dialog box, choose the Location where the workbook will be saved. Select the Documents directory.

  4. In the Save to OneDrive window, save the Excel file as “My_Bicycle_Shop_Sales”.

    Note: Excel will, by default, save the workbook with an .xlsx file extension.

  5. Close the Excel workbook.

Step 2: Opening an Excel Workbook

  1. Relaunch Excel from your Office.com account using the method in Part 1 of the lab.

  2. To open a saved Excel workbook, select the File tab in the top left of the menu bar then select Open in the left pane.

  3. The “My_Bicycle_Shop_Sales” workbook saved in the previous step should be in the Recent files list. Click on the workbook to open it.

Part 3: Working with Cells, Rows, and Columns

An Excel worksheet is made up of a set of rows and columns. Rows are identified by numbers and columns are identified by letters. An Excel worksheet has 16,384 columns and 1,048,576 rows.

The rectangular box at the intersection of a row and column is called a cell. Each cell is identified by its column and row coordinates, such as A1 or B2. The coordinates of the active cell are indicated in the Cell Name Box above column A. A cell reference is always the column letter followed by the row number. Values, which include numbers and letters, can be typed into cells. Cells can also contain mathematical formulas and functions for performing calculations.

Step 1: Changing the Active Cell

  1. Click inside cell A1 at the top left corner of the worksheet. Notice the cell now has a border around it and the cell coordinates are displayed above column A in the Cell Name Box.

    This indicates that cell A1 is the active cell. Only one cell is active at a time and data can only be entered into the currently active cell.

  2. Now, click into cell B2. Note that this cell is now the active cell with a border and its reference coordinates are in the Cell Name Box above column A.

What happens if you type the coordinates of a cell directly into the Cell Name Box?
Answer Area
That cell becomes the active cell and is selected.

Step 2: Selecting Columns and Rows

As previously mentioned, columns are labeled with letters at the top of each column—this is called the header. To select an entire column, click the letter at the top of the column.

You can resize a column by clicking the dividing line at the right of a column header and dragging it left or right.

  1. Click the header for Column A to select the entire column. Column A is now highlighted.

  1. Move the cursor to the line to the right of the Column A header so that the cursor turns into a double headed arrow. Drag the cursor right to widen Column A.

  2. Now click the row heading for Row 1 so that the row is highlighted.

  1. Move the cursor to the line dividing the headers of Row 1 and Row 2 and drag it down to make Row 1 taller.

How can you select more than one column or row?
Answer Area
Answers may vary. Common methods are:
(1) Select the column or row and drag the mouse to select the desired number of columns or rows.
(2) Select the column or row and hold down the Shift key on the keyboard and press the directional keys to select additional columns or rows.
(3) Typing column or row names in the Cell Name Box, separated by a colon (e.g., A:D or 1:5).

Step 3: Adding Rows and Columns

To add a row or column, click on a header or a cell, and then go up to the toolbar, or ribbon, and find the Insert button in the Cells group. Click the arrow below Insert and in the drop-down menu, select Insert Sheet Rows or Insert Sheet Columns.

You can add multiple rows or columns by selecting the number of rows or columns you want to add and using the same method as above. The number of rows or columns added will be the same and the number that you have selected. For example, selecting 4 rows and clicking Insert Sheet Rows will add 4 rows to the worksheet above those you have selected.

  1. Try adding a few rows and columns to the worksheet.

Note: Depending on the size of your window, to see the cells group, it may be necessary to expand the Excel toolbar by clicking on the small down arrow at the far-right edge of the toolbar, shown below.

To delete a row or column, you follow the same process, except click the arrow under the Delete button in the Cells group and then select Delete Sheet Rows or Delete Sheet Columns.

  1. Try deleting a few rows and columns from the worksheet.

Step 4: Selecting a Range of Cells

Click cell B2 and drag the mouse to select the cell range of B2 through C6. The selected range is highlighted; the active cell is the top left cell in the range but is not highlighted.

What is the result of typing A1:C5 in the Cell Name Box?
Answer Area
All cells in the range of A1 through C5 are selected.

Step 5: Selecting the Entire Sheet

To select the entire worksheet, click the triangle at the top left corner of the worksheet.

The entire worksheet can also be selected by with the keyboard shortcut Ctrl+A.

Part 4: Managing Excel Worksheets

When you open a new Excel file, you are creating a workbook with one worksheet, titled by default Sheet1. The name of the worksheet appears in a tab at the bottom left of the window. You can add as many worksheets as you need to hold your data to a workbook. The tabs at the bottom left of the window are used to switch between worksheets and to insert new worksheets into a workbook.

Step 1: Adding and Deleting Worksheets

  1. When you launch Excel the default worksheet is named Sheet1. You can add a worksheet by clicking the plus + button next to the worksheet tabs. A new worksheet is added with the default name of Sheet2.
  2. You can also add a worksheet using the shortcut SHIFT + F11. If you do this to add a third worksheet, Excel names this worksheet Sheet3.
  3. You can delete a worksheet by right-clicking on the worksheet name and selecting Delete from the pop-up menu. Using this procedure, delete Sheet 3.
    Note that once you delete a worksheet you cannot recover it.
  4. Note: Some versions of some spreadsheet tools, including Excel, have a Version History feature that you may be able to use to recover data by restoring an old version or copying and pasting. This varies between programs and depends on whether the deleted data was previously saved.

Step 2: Renaming a New Worksheet

  1. To rename a worksheet, right click (or use your operating system’s shortcut for right click) on the worksheet tab and select Rename from the pop-up menu.
    1. Right-click on Sheet1, rename the worksheet “Sales Data.”
    2. Right-click on Sheet2, rename the worksheet “Historical Sales.”
What happens to the data in a worksheet when the worksheet is deleted?
Answer Area
The data is lost, unless it can be recovered from a previous version of the document.

Step 2: Formatting Cell Contents

There are many formatting options available under the home tab of the toolbar.

The toolbar may need to be expanded to see all of the formatting options as shown below. See instructions above to expand the toolbar if you don’t see all these options.

The Font group has options for formatting the text font: font style, font size, formatting, and font color.

The Alignment group has options for alignment and indentation of cell text, merging and un-merging cells, and wrapping text in a cell.

The Numbers group has options for correctly formatting numeric cell contents to currency, numbers, dates, percentages, comma style, etc.

You can also access formatting options for cells by right clicking a cell and by using the shortcut key Control + 1 (in Windows).

  1. Bold the column headers in Row 1 by selecting the cell range A1 through D1 and then, with the cell range selected, click the bold “B” button in the Font group or press CTRL+B on the keyboard.
  2. Change the alignment of the values in the Product_Category column from left justified to right justified by selecting the cell range B2 through B4 and then clicking the Align Text Right button in the Alignment group.
  3. Change the values in the Revenue column to currency by selecting the cell range D2 through D4 and then, with the cell range selected, click the dollar sign “$” button in the Number group.
